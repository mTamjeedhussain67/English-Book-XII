#  UNIT 1.1: TRUSTWORTHINESS AND TOLERANCE

**Study Notes & Practice Material**
**Based on "The Devoted Friend" by Oscar Wilde**
*Premium Study Resource for Students*

---

##  CHAPTER SUMMARY

### Overview
This unit focuses on developing reading comprehension and critical thinking skills through Oscar Wilde's satirical short story, **"The Devoted Friend."** The story explores themes of friendship, exploitation, selfishness, and the difference between words and actions.

### Plot Summary
- **Introduction:** Little Hans, an honest gardener, lives alone in a tiny cottage with a beautiful garden full of flowers. His "most devoted friend" is Big Hugh the Miller, a wealthy man who constantly takes flowers, herbs, and fruit from Hans's garden. The Miller preaches that "real friends should have everything in common," but never gives anything in return.
- **Winter Hardship:** During spring, summer, and autumn, Hans is happy working in his garden. In winter, Hans suffers from cold and hunger, with no flowers to sell. The Miller refuses to visit Hans in winter, claiming that "when people are in trouble they should be left alone."
- **Spring Returns – The Exploitation Begins:** When spring arrives, the Miller visits Hans, promising to give him his old, broken wheelbarrow. In exchange, the Miller manipulates Hans into giving him a plank of wood for his barn roof and filling a large basket with primroses.
- **Increasing Demands:** The Miller repeatedly asks Hans to do chores: carrying flour to market, mending the barn roof, driving sheep to the mountain. Each time, Hans hesitates but agrees because he believes the Miller is his "best friend."
- **The Tragic Ending:** One stormy night, the Miller comes to Hans's door, asking him to fetch the doctor for his injured son. Hans agrees, even though the Miller refuses to lend his lantern. Hans gets lost in the storm, falls into a pool on the moor, and drowns. At Hans's funeral, the Miller acts as the chief mourner but complains about the wheelbarrow he never actually gave.

##  COMPREHENSION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Short Answer Questions

**Q1: How does the friendship between Hans and the Miller seem odd from the beginning?**
**A:** The friendship is one-sided. The Miller constantly takes flowers, herbs, and fruit from Hans's garden but never gives anything in return. He preaches that "real friends should have everything in common," yet he keeps all his wealth (flour, cows, sheep) to himself while Hans suffers.

**Q2: Why did Hans sell his wheelbarrow?**
**A:** Hans sold his wheelbarrow because winter was very hard for him. He had no money to buy bread, so he first sold his silver buttons, then his silver chain, then his big pipe, and finally his wheelbarrow to survive.

**Q3: In what way does the Miller exploit Hans with the promise of giving him his wheelbarrow?**
**A:** The Miller uses the promise of the broken wheelbarrow to manipulate Hans into giving him a plank of wood, filling a large basket with flowers, carrying flour to market, mending the barn roof, and driving sheep to the mountain—all without ever actually giving Hans the wheelbarrow.

**Q4: What final act of the Miller's selfishness leads to Hans' death?**
**A:** On a stormy night, the Miller asks Hans to fetch the doctor for his injured son but refuses to lend his lantern, saying it is "new" and might get damaged. Hans goes into the dark, dangerous storm without light, gets lost on the moor, and drowns.

**Q5: Hans' own simplicity and trustworthiness also contribute to his death. Do you agree? Justify.**
**A:** Yes. Hans blindly trusts the Miller's words despite clear evidence of exploitation. He repeatedly neglects his own needs and safety because he believes the Miller's promises. His inability to question false friendship and set boundaries makes him vulnerable to the Miller's manipulation.

**Q6: "False friends are worse than open enemies." Discuss this aphorism in the context of the story.**
**A:** An open enemy would not deceive Hans with kind words. The Miller, as a "false friend," gains Hans's trust and then exploits him. Hans never defends himself because he believes the Miller cares for him. This betrayal is more dangerous than open hostility because it disarms the victim emotionally.

**Q7: How would you deal if you have a friend like the Miller?**
**A:** (Sample answer) I would observe whether the friendship is reciprocal. If a friend only takes and never gives, or uses guilt and manipulation, I would set clear boundaries, communicate my feelings, and if necessary, distance myself. True friendship requires mutual respect and support.

**Q8: Write any five contrasting characteristics of Hans and the Miller.**

| Hans's Characteristics | Miller's Characteristics |
| :--- | :--- |
| Generous and selfless | Selfish and greedy |
| Simple and trusting | Cunning and manipulative |
| Hardworking and humble | Lazy and hypocritical |
| Acts with kindness | Speaks beautifully but acts selfishly |
| Values friendship sincerely | Uses "friendship" as a tool for exploitation |

### Descriptive Questions (Long Answers)

**Q1: Analyze the character of the Miller in "The Devoted Friend". How does he use the concept of friendship for his own selfish gains?**
**A:** Hugh, the rich Miller, represents hypocrisy and selfishness masked as friendship. He constantly takes from little Hans—demanding his wheelbarrow, his time, and his labor—while offering nothing but empty words and false promises in return. The Miller uses the ideology of "true friendship" to manipulate Hans, convincing him that serving others without expectation is a noble duty. His character critiques the exploitation of the poor by the rich under the guise of benevolence.

**Q2: What is the central theme of Oscar Wilde's "The Devoted Friend"? Discuss how the story serves as a critique of hypocrisy and unequal relationships.**
**A:** The central theme is the exploitation of innocence and the hypocrisy of conditional friendship. Wilde satirizes the Victorian ideals of morality by showing how the wealthy (the Miller) exploit the poor (Hans) while self-righteously preaching about generosity. The story highlights the danger of unequal relationships where one party constantly sacrifices while the other only benefits, ultimately leading to the tragic destruction of the devoted, naive friend.

### Key Quotes to Remember

| Quote | Speaker | Significance |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| "Real friends should have everything in common." | The Miller | Shows hypocrisy—he takes everything, gives nothing |
| "When people are in trouble they should be left alone." | The Miller | Excuse for abandoning Hans in winter |
| "Friendship never forgets." | The Miller | Ironic—he forgot Hans all winter |
| "One always suffers for being generous." | The Miller | Final ironic statement after causing Hans's death |

### Literary Devices Used
- **Irony:** The Miller claims to be generous but is selfish.
- **Satire:** Criticizes false morality and hypocritical friendship.
- **Symbolism:** The broken wheelbarrow = empty promises.
- **Contrast:** Hans's actions vs. Miller's words.

-  Always support answers with evidence from the text.
-  For "moral of the story" questions, focus on reciprocity in friendship.
-  When analyzing characters, compare words vs. actions.
